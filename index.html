Primary Source Analysis · 25 minutes
Why Did Bin Laden Attack the United States?
~25 min
Key idea before you read: Understanding why someone did something is not the same as agreeing with or excusing it. Historians must understand the motivations of historical actors — even when those actors did terrible things. Your job right now is to understand, not to agree.

Background: Bin Laden's Stated Grievances

Osama bin Laden was not silent about his reasons. He published statements and gave interviews throughout the 1990s. In 1998, he issued a formal religious declaration (called a fatwa) explaining why he believed attacking Americans was justified. His reasons were specific — not vague hatred, but grievances rooted in US foreign policy. Read the excerpts below.

Primary Source A — Bin Laden's 1998 Fatwa (Declaration of War)
"For over seven years the United States has been occupying the lands of Islam in the holiest of places, the Arabian Peninsula, plundering its riches, dictating to its rulers, humiliating its people, terrorizing its neighbors, and turning its bases in the Peninsula into a spearhead through which to fight the neighboring Muslim peoples."
— Osama bin Laden, "Jihad Against Jews and Crusaders," World Islamic Front Statement, February 23, 1998
Primary Source B — Bin Laden's "Letter to America" (2002)
"You are the nation who, rather than ruling by the Shariah of Allah in its Constitution and Laws, choose to invent your own laws as you will and desire. You separate religion from your policies, contradicting the pure nature which affirms Absolute Authority to the Lord and your Creator... You are the nation that permits usury, which is forbidden by Islam, but through which you enable the Jews to control your economy... You have starved the Muslims of Iraq, where children die every day... You have supported the Jews in their idea that Jerusalem is their eternal capital, and agreed to move your Embassy there."
— Osama bin Laden, "Letter to America," November 2002

Bin Laden's Four Main Grievances — Explained

These are the reasons he stated most consistently. Read each one carefully.

Grievance What He Claimed Historical Context
1. US Military in Saudi Arabia After the Gulf War (1991), the US kept troops in Saudi Arabia — land he considered sacred Islamic territory. Around 5,000 US troops remained in Saudi Arabia after Operation Desert Storm. This was bin Laden's most consistent and personal grievance.
2. US Support for Israel The US backed Israel politically, economically, and militarily while Palestinians suffered. The US provides billions in military aid to Israel annually. Bin Laden invoked Palestinian suffering repeatedly as a rallying cause.
3. US Support for Arab Dictators The US propped up authoritarian governments in Saudi Arabia, Egypt, and elsewhere, preventing true Islamic governance. The US backed these regimes for strategic/oil reasons throughout the Cold War and after, even as they suppressed their own citizens.
4. Iraq Sanctions US-led sanctions after the Gulf War killed hundreds of thousands of Iraqi civilians, especially children. UN sanctions on Iraq (1990–2003) did cause severe humanitarian suffering. UN officials estimate hundreds of thousands of civilian deaths, especially children, from lack of medicine and food.

US Response · 15 minutes
The War on Terror Begins
~15 min

The United States responded to 9/11 with a series of rapid military and political decisions. These decisions shaped American foreign policy for the next two decades — and are still felt today.

Sept 14, 2001
Authorization for Use of Military Force (AUMF) — Congress gives the President broad authority to use military force against those responsible for 9/11. Only one member of Congress votes against it.
Oct 7, 2001
US invades Afghanistan — Targeting al-Qaeda and the Taliban government that sheltered them. The war lasts 20 years, until the US withdrawal in August 2021.
Oct 26, 2001
USA PATRIOT Act signed into law — Dramatically expands government surveillance powers. Passed with almost no debate — many members of Congress admitted they hadn't read it.
Nov 2001
Department of Homeland Security created — A new cabinet agency to coordinate domestic security. TSA, airport screening, and "threat levels" become part of American life.
March 20, 2003
US invades Iraq — President Bush claims Iraq has weapons of mass destruction and links to al-Qaeda. Both claims later proven false. The war lasts until 2011 and costs over 4,400 American lives and hundreds of thousands of Iraqi lives.

Looking at the timeline, what pattern do you notice about how the US responded? What values or fears seem to be driving these decisions?

Civil Liberties · 20 minutes
Security vs. Freedom — The PATRIOT Act
~20 min

After 9/11, the government argued it needed new powers to prevent future attacks. The USA PATRIOT Act (2001) and later revelations about NSA surveillance programs raised a fundamental question: How much freedom are Americans willing to trade for security?

Key Provisions of the PATRIOT Act

📞 Phone & Internet Surveillance

Government can monitor phone calls, emails, and internet activity with fewer restrictions than before. "Roving wiretaps" allow surveillance across multiple devices.

📚 Library & Business Records

FBI can secretly demand library records, bookstore purchases, business records — without telling the person they are investigating.

🏠 "Sneak and Peek" Searches

Government can search a home or business without immediately telling the owner. Notification can be delayed for months.

🚫 National Security Letters

FBI can demand information from businesses with no court approval. Recipients are legally forbidden from telling anyone they received one.

2013 Revelation: NSA whistleblower Edward Snowden revealed that the US government was secretly collecting phone records from every American — not just terrorism suspects — and could access private data from Google, Facebook, Apple, and other companies. This program had been operating secretly since 2001.
